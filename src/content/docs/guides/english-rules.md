# English Syllable Counting Rules

Counting syllables in English can be tricky due to irregular spelling and pronunciation. Here are the main rules to follow for poetry, haikus, and general writing.

## 1. Each syllable has at least one vowel sound

- English vowels: a, e, i, o, u, and sometimes y.
- Example: _computer_ → com-pu-ter → 3 syllables

## 2. Diphthongs and triphthongs count as one syllable

- Two or three vowel sounds pronounced together in one syllable.
- Examples:
  - _coin_ → 1 syllable
  - _beautiful_ → beau-ti-ful → 3 syllables

## 3. Consonants separate syllables

- Consonants between vowels usually start a new syllable.
- Example: _happen_ → hap-pen → 2 syllables

## 4. Silent “e” at the end is not counted

- Example: _make_ → make → 1 syllable

## 5. Suffixes -ed and -es can add a syllable

- Depends on pronunciation:
  - _wanted_ → wan-ted → +1 syllable
  - _wishes_ → wish-es → +1 syllable
  - _played_ → played → 1 syllable

## 6. “Y” can function as a vowel

- Example: _happy_, _my_ → counts as a vowel sound

## 7. Compound words are divided by their parts

- Example: _football_ → foot-ball → 2 syllables

## 8. Reductions in spoken English

- Optional, difficult to compute automatically.
- Example: _interesting_ → in-te-res-ting → 3–4 syllables depending on pronunciation

### Notes

- These rules are mostly orthographic approximations.
- Exceptions exist due to irregular English spelling.
