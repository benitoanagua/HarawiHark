# Spanish Syllable Counting Rules

Counting syllables in Spanish is more regular than in English, but poetry and accents require attention. Here are the main rules.

## 1. Each syllable has at least one vowel

- Vowels: a, e, i, o, u, á, é, í, ó, ú, ü
- Example: _casa_ → ca-sa → 2 syllables

## 2. Diphthongs

- A combination of a weak vowel (i, u) with a strong vowel (a, e, o) or two weak vowels pronounced together counts as one syllable.
- Example: _tierra_ → tie-rra → 2 syllables

## 3. Triphthongs

- A sequence of three vowels (closed + open + closed) pronounced together counts as one syllable.
- Example: _Uruguay_ → u-ru-guay → 3 syllables

## 4. Hiatos

- Two vowels pronounced separately count as two syllables.
- Pay attention to hiatos with accents, which break diphthongs.
- Example: _país_ → pa-ís → 2 syllables

## 5. Consonants between vowels

- Consonants generally separate syllables, except certain inseparable clusters (pl, tr, bl...).
- Example: _comer_ → co-mer → 2 syllables

## 6. “H” does not break syllables or diphthongs

- Example: _ahijado_ → a-hi-ja-do → 4 syllables

## 7. “Y” at the end can function as a vowel

- Example: _hoy_, _rey_ → counts as one syllable

### Notes

- Sinalefa (merging vowels between words) is only relevant in poetry.
- These rules provide a reliable method for counting syllables in Spanish texts.
